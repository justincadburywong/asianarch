The Asian American Research Center on Health (ARCH) includes prominent
 researchers, community leaders, and clinicians in the San Francisco Bay Area.
 The Center will maintain an open and collaborative environment to carry out
 the vision, which is to be a leader in Asian American health research to
 promote health and health equity in partnership with Asian American
 communities. ARCH is currently supported by the UCSF Helen Diller Family 
 Comprehensive Cancer Center, the UCSF Department of Medicine, the UCSF Medical
 Effectiveness Research Center, and the Jenkins Bernen Endowment.
                                             Tung Nguyen, MD, ARCH Director
                                             Alka Kanaya, MD, ARCH Co-Director

Congratulations to ARCH investigators for their recent publications and presentations!


    • JiWon Choi, Melinda Bender, Yoshimi Fukuoka and co-authors published “Factors Associated with Underestimation of Weight Status among Caucasian, Latino, Filipino, and Korean Americans--DiLH Survey” in Ethnicity & Disease.

    • Jyu-Lin Chen published in a manuscript in Clinical Pediatric reporting on an 8 weekly small group healthy weight management intervention for overweight or obese Chinese American children.

    • Janice Tsoh, Nancy Burke, Ching Wong, Angela Sun, Stephen McPhee, Tung Nguyen and colleagues published “A Social Network Family-Focused Intervention to Promote Smoking Cessation in Chinese and Vietnamese American Male Smokers: A Feasibility Study” in Nicotine & Tobacco Research.

 
    • Van M. Ta Park, Tung Nguyen, and colleagues published a mixed methods pilot study exploring postpartum traditions, mental health, and health-seeking considerations among Vietnamese American women. Dr. Park and colleagues also pubished a pilot qualitative study of Asian Indian mothers and their perspectives of postpartum depression, two emerging themes were culture-specific post-partum practices and ceremonies and their role in maternal-infant postpartum recovery and maternal mental health help-seeking behavior.
