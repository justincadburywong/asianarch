Congratulations to ARCH investigators for their recent publications!

Alka Kanaya and her team recently published a manuscript in Atherosclerosis entitled “Comparing coronary artery calcium among U.S. South Asians with four racial/ethnic groups: the MASALA and MESA studies.”  They found that South Asian men had similar high coronary artery calcium (CAC) burden as White men but higher than African Americans, Latinos and Chinese Americans.  South Asian women had similar CAC burden as White, African American, Latina and Chinese women but had a somewhat higher CAC burden at older age. 

 

Grace Yoo, Rena Pasick and their team published a systematic review in Supportive Care in Cancer entitled “Breast cancer and coping among women of color: a systematic review of the literature.”  Their systematic review included 33 studies examining what is known about coping in breast cancer among racial/ethnic minorities and identified strengths and gaps in the literature.

 

Janice Tsoh and colleagues from the CDC and Lowell Community Health Center in Massachusetts recently published “Decreased Smoking Disparities among Vietnamese and Cambodian Communities — Racial and Ethnic Approaches to Community Health (REACH) Project, 2002–2006” in the Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report.  In the REACH Vietnamese and Cambodian communities, the prevalence of current smoking was significantly lower and the quit ratio was significantly increased – greater in these REACH communities than in the United States at large.  The results of this project highlight the potential effectiveness of community-level interventions!

Janice Tsoh also published her study in collaboration with the Santa Clara County Public Health Department “Determinants of exposure to secondhand smoke among Vietnamese adults: California Vietnamese Adult Tobacco Use Survey, 2007-2008” in Preventing Chronic Disease. They found that 30% of Vietnamese non-smokers were exposed to secondhand smoke (SHS) at home, 8% at work, 52% in bars, and 67% on a college campus. This highlights the need for better policies to reduce secondhand smoke exposure and better enforcement of such policies in public settings.

Scarlett Lin Gomez and her research colleagues had 2 recent publications using the California Cancer Registry.

  • First, their paper “Prostate cancer risk profiles of Asian-American men: disentangling the effects of immigration status and race/ethnicity” was published in The Journal of Urology.  Six Californian Asian American subgroups (US-born Chinese, foreign-born Chinese, US-born Japanese, foreign -orn Japanese, foreign-born Filipino and foreign-born Vietnamese) were more likely to have an unfavorable risk profile compared to non-Hispanic white men that was not attributable to later stage at diagnosis.
  • Second, in a manuscript published in The American Journal of Gastroenterology, Scarlett and team members report on “Colorectal Cancer Incidence in Asian Populations in California: Effect of Nativity and Neighborhood-Level Factors.”  Incidence of colorectal cancer (CRC) was lowest among foreign-born South Asians and highest among foreign-born Japanese.  Women in all Asian subgroups except Japanese and men in all Asian subgroups except Japanese and US-born Chinese had lower CRC incidence than non-Hispanic Whites.  Incidence of CRC was inversely associated with level of ethnic enclave among Asians.

 

Nancy Burke and her colleagues at the Fred Hutchinson Cancer Center in Washington published their study “Protecting our Khmer daughters: ghosts of the past, uncertain futures, and the human papillomavirus vaccine” in Ethnicity and Health. This was a qualitative study with 25 Cambodian mothers of HPV-vaccine eligible daughters and shed light on the prioritization of protective health practices among mothers with a focus on historical issues such as their surviving genocide.
